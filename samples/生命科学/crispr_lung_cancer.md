# CRISPR Reverses Chemoresistance in Lung Cancer Treatment

## Gene Editing Restores Drug Sensitivity in Resistant Tumors

A transformative study published in Nature Medicine (2025) by researchers at the Gene Editing Institute at ChristianaCare has demonstrated that CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing can effectively reverse chemotherapy resistance in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), offering new hope for patients who have exhausted conventional treatment options.

## The Chemoresistance Crisis

Chemoresistance — the ability of cancer cells to survive and proliferate despite drug treatment — is the single greatest obstacle to successful cancer therapy. In non-small cell lung cancer, which accounts for 85% of all lung cancer cases:

- Approximately 60% of NSCLC patients develop resistance to first-line platinum-based chemotherapy within 6 months
- Second-line therapies achieve response rates of only 10-15% in resistant patients
- Median overall survival for chemoresistant NSCLC patients is 8-10 months

The molecular mechanisms of resistance are multifaceted:

1. **Drug efflux pumps**: Overexpression of ABCB1 (MDR1) and ABCC1 actively pump chemotherapy drugs out of cancer cells
2. **DNA repair enhancement**: Upregulation of ERCC1 and BRCA1 enables cancer cells to repair cisplatin-induced DNA damage
3. **Apoptosis evasion**: Mutations in TP53 and overexpression of BCL-2 family proteins prevent programmed cell death
4. **Epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT)**: Cancer cells acquire stem-like properties that confer multidrug resistance

## The CRISPR-TIL Approach

The ChristianaCare team developed an innovative approach that combines tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy with CRISPR gene editing:

### Step 1: TIL Isolation
Tumor tissue is surgically resected from the patient and dissociated into single cells. Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (T cells that have naturally migrated into the tumor) are isolated and expanded ex vivo using high-dose IL-2 (6000 IU/mL) over 2-3 weeks, expanding the population from millions to billions of cells.

### Step 2: CRISPR Gene Editing
The expanded TIL cells are edited using CRISPR-Cas9 ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complexes to knock out the CISH gene:

- **Target gene**: CISH (Cytokine-inducible SH2-containing protein)
- **Function**: CISH is a negative regulator of T-cell receptor (TCR) signaling — it acts as a "brake" on T-cell activation
- **Delivery method**: Electroporation of Cas9 protein pre-complexed with guide RNA
- **Editing efficiency**: 92% allelic disruption
- **Off-target rate**: <0.1% (confirmed by whole-genome sequencing of edited cells)

### Step 3: Quality Control and Reinfusion
Edited TIL cells undergo rigorous quality control:

- Flow cytometry confirmation of CISH protein knockout (>90% of cells)
- Karyotype analysis to confirm genomic integrity
- Endotoxin and mycoplasma testing
- Functional assays confirming enhanced cytokine production

Patients receive lymphodepleting chemotherapy (cyclophosphamide + fludarabine) before TIL infusion, followed by IL-2 support.

## Mechanism of Action

CISH knockout enhances anti-tumor immunity through multiple mechanisms:

- **Increased T-cell proliferation**: CISH-deficient TIL cells show 3.5-fold increased expansion upon antigen encounter
- **Enhanced cytokine production**: IFN-gamma secretion increases by 280%, improving the tumor microenvironment
- **Improved tumor killing**: Cytotoxic activity against tumor spheroids increases 4.2-fold
- **Overcoming resistance**: Edited TIL cells demonstrate the ability to kill chemotherapy-resistant tumor cells that express PD-L1 and other immune evasion markers

## Clinical Trial Results

The Phase I/II clinical trial (NCT05838249) enrolled 32 patients with advanced, chemoresistant NSCLC:

### Patient Characteristics
- Median age: 62 years (range 41-78)
- All patients had progressed on at least two prior lines of therapy
- 78% had adenocarcinoma, 22% had squamous cell carcinoma
- PD-L1 expression: 0% in 34%, 1-49% in 28%, 50%+ in 38%

### Efficacy Results

| Endpoint | Result |
|----------|--------|
| Objective response rate (ORR) | 41.2% (14/34 evaluable) |
| Complete responses (CR) | 5.9% (2/34) |
| Partial responses (PR) | 35.3% (12/34) |
| Stable disease (SD) | 32.4% (11/34) |
| Disease control rate (DCR) | 73.5% |
| Median progression-free survival | 7.8 months (95% CI: 5.2-10.4) |
| Median overall survival | 15.3 months (95% CI: 11.6-19.0) |
| 12-month overall survival rate | 62% |

### Safety Profile
- Treatment-related adverse events (Grade 3+): 26.5%
- Most common: Neutropenia (20.6%), thrombocytopenia (14.7%)
- Immune-related adverse events: Colitis (5.9%), pneumonitis (2.9%)
- Treatment-related deaths: 0%
- No CRISPR-related safety signals detected

## Comparison with Existing Therapies

The CRISPR-TIL results compare favorably with current standard-of-care options for chemoresistant NSCLC:

- **Docetaxel monotherapy**: ORR 10%, median OS 8 months
- **Docetaxel + ramucirumab**: ORR 23%, median OS 10.5 months
- **Pembrolizumab (PD-L1 >= 50%)**: ORR 30-35%, median OS 12-14 months
- **CRISPR-TIL**: ORR 41.2%, median OS 15.3 months

## Future Directions

The research team is pursuing several next steps:

1. **Phase III trial**: A 200-patient randomized trial comparing CRISPR-TIL to standard-of-care is planned for 2026
2. **Combination therapy**: Testing CRISPR-TIL in combination with anti-PD-1 antibodies for potential synergistic effects
3. **Multi-gene editing**: Simultaneously knocking out CISH and PD-1 to further enhance TIL function
4. **Other indications**: Expanding the platform to melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, and triple-negative breast cancer
5. **Manufacturing optimization**: Reducing the 4-week production timeline to 2 weeks through process automation
